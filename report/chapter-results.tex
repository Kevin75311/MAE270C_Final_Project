\section{Setup and Experimental Matrix}

All experiments use the default configuration in \texttt{config.py}
(the \emph{non-aggressive} cost gains, $\alpha_1 = 5\times10^{-4}$,
$\gamma_1 = 10^6$, $\gamma_2 = 0.1$) with the Dirichlet boundary
condition held fixed across every run, so that the only quantities
varied are the solver mode, the spatial dimensionality, and the probe
(SAR) geometry.
Table~\ref{tab:sim-params} summarizes the key parameters.

The clinical healthy-tissue safety threshold is typically
$42$--$45\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$; we adopt
$T_{\text{safe}} = \SI{50}{\degreeCelsius}$ here so that complete ablation
of the default geometry remains feasible, since at
$\SI{45}{\degreeCelsius}$ the hard-constrained problem caps near $76\%$
ablation for this probe placement.

Every reported cost is evaluated at the free final time $t_f^\star$ ---
the first step at which the entire tumor satisfies $\Omega_d \geq 1$ ---
not at the $\SI{300}{\second}$ horizon cap. Because the running cost is
strictly positive and $\gamma_2 > 0$, the transversality condition
$\mathcal{H}(t_f) = -\gamma_2$ is infeasible, so the optimum sits on the
active terminal (ablation) constraint: running past $t_f^\star$ only
wastes cost.

The full sweep is the Cartesian product of three solver modes
$\{\text{open-loop},\,\text{indirect},\,\text{direct}\}$, two
dimensionalities $\{\text{2-D},\,\text{3-D}\}$, and three probe
geometries $\{\text{point},\,\text{line},\,\text{dipole}\}$, giving
$18$ runs, executed in parallel via a thread pool of subprocesses
(\texttt{run\_all.py}).
Fifteen completed at the default $50^3$ resolution. The three
\emph{direct, 3-D} runs did not converge there --- single-shooting
\gls{slsqp} re-integrates the full $50^3 = 125{,}000$-voxel \gls{pde} at
every finite-difference function evaluation, and after more than nine
hours per run the optimizer had not converged. They were rerun at a
coarse $20^3$ grid, where they converge only to a degenerate
``do-nothing'' optimum, for the cost-scaling reason discussed in
Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}.

\section{Solver Behavior}
\label{sec:solver-behavior}

This section walks through each solver on the canonical 2-D point-probe
phantom; the cross-cutting comparison follows in
Section~\ref{sec:comparison}.

\paragraph{Open-loop baseline.}
The open-loop mode applies a fixed bang-bang schedule (full power until
the tumor ablates, then off) with no optimization
(Figure~\ref{fig:openloop}). The applicator drives the tumor center well
above $\SI{60}{\degreeCelsius}$ and fully ablates the $\SI{8}{\mm}$ tumor,
while the peak healthy-tissue temperature stays at
$\SI{47.6}{\degreeCelsius}$, within the
$T_{\text{safe}} = \SI{50}{\degreeCelsius}$ limit. The fixed schedule
ablates in $t_f^\star = \SI{92}{\second}$ at the highest energy
deposition of any solver.

\paragraph{Indirect (PMP).}
The indirect solver iterates forward-state / backward-adjoint sweeps and
projects the Pontryagin control law~\eqref{eq:pontryagin} onto
$[0, P_{\max}]$, relaxed with $\beta = 0.15$.
Because the energy term $\alpha_1\|P\|^2$ makes full power wasteful, the
optimizer trades \emph{time} for \emph{energy}: it spreads a gentler power
profile over a longer horizon (Figure~\ref{fig:indirect}\subref{fig:ind-power}),
ablating in $t_f^\star = \SI{224}{\second}$ versus the open-loop
$\SI{92}{\second}$, but cutting the energy cost from
$J_{\text{energy}} = 115$ to $82$ and the total cost from $125$ to $106$.
The healthy-tissue traces
(Figure~\ref{fig:indirect}\subref{fig:ind-temp}) preserve the
$T_{\text{safe}}$ margin, and the cost breakdown
(Figure~\ref{fig:indirect}\subref{fig:ind-cost}) is dominated by the
energy and $\gamma_2 t_f$ terms with zero ablation and zero
healthy-overheat penalty.

\paragraph{Direct (SLSQP).}
The direct single-shooting NLP optimizes the full power vector directly
with \texttt{scipy} \gls{slsqp} (Figure~\ref{fig:direct2}).
On the point probe it converges to essentially the same energy/time
trade-off as the indirect solver
($J_{\text{total}} = 110$ vs.\ $106$, $t_f^\star = \SI{265}{\second}$),
confirming that the two optimizers reach the same optimum from different
directions when the SAR geometry is the same.
Its lowest cost overall is achieved with the more focused dipole probe
($J_{\text{total}} = 49.7$), examined in Section~\ref{sec:comparison}.

\paragraph{Solver effect.}
Restricting to the 2-D point-probe case isolates the solver from geometry
(Table~\ref{tab:solver-comparison}). All three solvers fully ablate the
tumor while keeping healthy tissue below $T_{\text{safe}}$. The two true
optimizers reduce total cost by $\approx 12$--$15\%$ relative to open-loop
by trading a longer treatment time for substantially lower energy
deposition --- precisely the energy/time trade-off engineered by the
$\alpha_1\|P\|^2$ term. Open-loop, which runs essentially at full power,
gives the shortest treatment but the highest energy.

\paragraph{Probe geometry.}
Two robust findings emerge from the probe sweep. First, \emph{point and
line are indistinguishable in 2-D}: in an $x$--$y$ slice the line source
is oriented along the out-of-plane $z$ axis, so its in-plane footprint is
the same radial Gaussian as the point source, and every 2-D point/line
pair in Table~\ref{tab:full-sweep} is numerically identical. They diverge
only in 3-D, where the line source deposits energy along its whole active
length. Second, \emph{the dipole pattern is most efficient in 2-D}: the
$\sin^2\theta$ toroidal near-field concentrates SAR more tightly on the
tumor, so every dipole run achieves the lowest cost of its mode
(e.g.\ direct 2-D: $49.7$ vs.\ $110.1$ for point/line). The same focusing
becomes a liability in 3-D, where the tighter pattern cannot cover the full
tumor volume within the horizon, leaving $\approx 1.6\%$ un-ablated.
The three SAR geometries are shown in 3-D in Figure~\ref{fig:sar-3d}: the
point source is an isotropic Gaussian blob, the line source extends a
cylindrical heating zone along the probe axis, and the dipole produces the
characteristic $\sin^2\theta$ toroidal pattern with a radial peak offset
from the axis.

\paragraph{2-D versus 3-D feasibility.}
The transition to 3-D is where the safety constraint starts to bite
(Figure~\ref{fig:3d}). In 2-D all nine runs are feasible with
$2$--$3\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$ of margin. In 3-D, out-of-plane conduction
requires more power to ablate the whole tumor \emph{volume}, and with the
Dirichlet walls $\SI{2.5}{\cm}$ away several configurations push healthy
tissue past $T_{\text{safe}}$. The \emph{line} probe overheats worst
(open-loop $\SI{75.3}{\degreeCelsius}$, indirect $\SI{67.3}{\degreeCelsius}$)
as heating along its full active length spills into healthy tissue; the
\emph{dipole} mildly overheats ($\approx \SI{56}{\degreeCelsius}$)
\emph{and} cannot finish the volume ($98.4\%$); only the \emph{point}
probe stays fully feasible across open-loop and indirect.
Because these runs use the soft $\alpha_2$ penalty rather than a hard
constraint, the optimizer is \emph{permitted} to exceed $T_{\text{safe}}$
when the ablation reward outweighs the penalty --- the infeasible 3-D
entries are exactly the cases where it chooses to. Promoting the safety
limit to a hard path constraint is the natural fix
(Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}).

\section{Summary of Findings}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Optimizers beat the baseline in 2-D.} Indirect and
    direct cut total cost by $12$--$15\%$ over open-loop by trading
    treatment time for energy, exactly as the $\alpha_1\|P\|^2$ term
    intends. All 2-D runs are feasible.
    \item \textbf{Geometry matters more than the solver.} The focused
    dipole SAR pattern halves the 2-D cost relative to the point/line
    Gaussian; point and line are identical in 2-D by construction.
    \item \textbf{3-D exposes the soft-constraint weakness.} The line and
    dipole sources overheat healthy tissue in 3-D because
    $T \leq T_{\text{safe}}$ is only soft-penalized; only the point probe
    stays feasible.
    \item \textbf{Solver scalability splits the methods.} The adjoint
    (indirect) method scales to 3-D in under an hour; dense direct
    single-shooting does not at $50^3$ (unconverged after nine hours), and
    only converges once the grid is coarsened to $20^3$.
    \item \textbf{A genuine optimizer exposes a mis-scaled weight.} At
    $20^3$ the direct solver converges to the \emph{do-nothing} optimum,
    because $\gamma_1$ was calibrated to the 2-D area integral and is
    $\approx 60\times$ too small for the 3-D volume integral. Cost weights
    must be re-scaled by voxel volume for 3-D.
\end{enumerate}
